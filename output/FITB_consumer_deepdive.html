1 · The one-line story

Consumer & Small Business Banking is Fifth Third's profit and funding engine. In Q4-2025 it generated $1,026M of net interest income — 67% of the entire bank's NII — plus $311M of fee income, for $608M of pretax profit, the largest of any segment (Commercial was $445M). The deposits this franchise gathers fund the whole balance sheet; the loans it makes earn a blended 5.91%. Growth is skewing to the high-yield, high-growth products (auto +11%, home equity +16%) and to the Southeast.

2 · The loan book — balances, finance charges & yields

Consumer is $49B of loans (~40% of the bank's $123B book), throwing off $2,865M of interest income (finance charges) in FY2025. The mix matters: the credit-card book is tiny ($1.7B, 3.5% of consumer loans) but yields 14.34% — Fifth Third is a deposit/debit-led consumer bank, not a card lender.

3 · The fee engine — interchange & deposit charges

Consumer banking revenue was $571M in FY2025. Per the 10-K it "consists primarily of interchange fees earned when consumer credit & debit cards are processed through card-association networks, monthly service charges on consumer deposit accounts, and other deposit-related charges." The FY25 rise was driven by higher deposit/overdraft fees. Debit interchange is Durbin-capped (21c + 0.05% of ticket + 1c fraud-prevention (regulated debit)) because Fifth Third is over $10B in assets — so this line grows with card volume and account count, not price.

4 · The deposit franchise (why NII is so high)

The segment's NII is outsized because it owns the deposits. Bank-wide average deposits were $168B, the loan-to-core-deposit ratio is 72%, and demand deposits grew 4% YoY. Under funds-transfer pricing, the consumer segment is credited for supplying this low-cost funding to the rest of the bank — which is why CSBB shows $1,026M of NII versus $581M for Commercial.

5 · Growth & distribution

1,000+ in 12 states. Net-new consumer households +2.5% YoY, led by the Southeast (+7%; Georgia +10%, Carolinas +9%). Home-equity is now #2 origination share in footprint (up from #4). The strategy is textbook: gather cheap deposits in a growing region, then lend into higher-yield consumer products.

6 · Consumer credit quality

Total net charge-offs were 1 bps in FY2025 (vs 0 bps in FY2024). In Q4-25, total NCOs were 0 bps and commercial only 0 bps — so consumer runs a bit hotter, as expected given the card/solar/other mix. Allowance for credit losses is a conservative 1.79% of loans. Credit is normalizing off unusually low levels, not deteriorating.

DEEP-DIVE TAKEAWAY
CSBB is the crown jewel: it funds the bank with cheap, growing deposits and lends into a well-diversified, 5.91%-yield consumer book. The upside levers are auto/home-equity growth and Southeast household gains; the watch items are deposit-fee dependence (Durbin-capped) and normalizing consumer credit. It is a spread-and-deposit story, not a credit-card story.
