CONSUMER BANKING · MULTI-YEAR TREND ANALYSIS
Fifth Third — FY2023 → FY2025
06 Jul 2026 · methods: horizontal · common-size · rate/volume variance
Methodology. This note uses the standard financial-statement-analysis toolkit: horizontal analysis (YoY change + 2-yr CAGR), vertical / common-size analysis (mix as % of total), and the rate/volume variance decomposition that banks are required to disclose in the 10-K "Analysis of Net Interest Income." Interest income = average balance × yield, so its change decomposes cleanly into a volume effect (balance growth) and a rate effect (yield change). Data: FITB FY2025 10-K.

3 · Vertical / common-size analysis — mix shift

Holding the book to 100%, the mix is tilting toward auto and solar and away from legacy "other consumer" — a deliberate move up the yield curve while keeping it secured.

4 · Yield trend (rate analysis)

Auto yields re-priced sharply higher (4.31%→5.62% over two years) as old low-rate paper rolled off; credit-card yield is stable ~14%; mortgage yields drift up slowly (long-duration, slow to reprice).

6 · Interchange vs. deposit-fee split — driver model

Fifth Third bundles interchange and deposit service charges into one line ("Consumer banking revenue", $571M FY25), so the split must be modeled, not read off. Per the 10-K this line "consists primarily of interchange fees... [and] monthly service charges on consumer deposit accounts." Debit interchange is capped by Durbin (Regulated debit cap: 21c + 0.05% of ticket + 1c fraud adjustment / transaction), so it scales with card volume, not price. Base case assumes interchange ≈ 45% of the line (≈ $257M); sensitivity:

7 · Deposit cost / funding trend

The other side of the ledger: blended interest-bearing deposit cost fell as the Fed peaked — e.g. interest-checking 3.28%→2.63%, money-market 2.90%→2.41% from FY24 to FY25 — the tailwind behind rising NIM. Non-interest-bearing demand deposits averaged $40.9B (-5.9% 2-yr CAGR) — free funding that lifts the consumer segment's spread.

TREND TAKEAWAY
Two-year story: consumer interest income compounded at 11.2% and the growth was 59% volume-driven (balance growth, led by auto & solar), not just rate-driven — a higher-quality growth signal. Deposit costs are now falling off the rate peak, widening the spread. Fee income is led by Wealth & Asset Management; the interchange line is Durbin-capped and volume-linked.

Methods: horizontal analysis (YoY, CAGR), vertical/common-size analysis, rate/volume variance decomposition (10-K standard). Source: FITB FY2025 Form 10-K (SEC EDGAR). Interchange split is a modeled estimate. Educational portfolio project — not investment advice.
